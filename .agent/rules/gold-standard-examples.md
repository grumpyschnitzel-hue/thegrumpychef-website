## Voice Checklist (Apply to Every Example)

Before any example is approved, it passes these tests:

- [ ] **Forest walk test:** Would Chris say this after a solo walk in the Black Forest?
- [ ] **60% capacity test:** Would an exhausted operator at 60% energy actually read this?
- [ ] **Dollar sign test:** Is there a specific number, metric, or outcome attached?
- [ ] **Recovery language:** Does it use "behavior leads belief," "no exceptions," "pre-decide" naturally?
- [ ] **Operator credibility:** Does it reference real kitchen experience, not theory?
- [ ] **No guru tone:** Does it sound like a peer who's been there, not a coach selling a system?
- [ ] **One idea:** Is there exactly one core idea, not three?

## Example 1: LinkedIn Text Post — BURNOUT

PLACEHOLDER — Replace with approved post

**Pain point:** Exhaustion / Burnout
**Awareness stage:** Problem Aware
**Hook type:** Failure / Vulnerability
**CTA destination:** Newsletter / Substack

---

I fell asleep standing up during Friday service.

Not metaphorically. Literally. My body shut down for 3 seconds while I was expediting. Nobody noticed. I played it off like I was reading a ticket.

That was year 4 of running my own place. I was doing 78-hour weeks and telling myself it was temporary. "Just until we stabilize." "Just until the new sous is trained." "Just until summer."

There was always a "just until."

Here's what I know now that I didn't know then: exhaustion doesn't announce itself. It shows up as mistakes you wouldn't normally make. Standards you let slide because you're too tired to fight. A temper that's shorter every week.

I didn't have a motivation problem. I had a system that required me to be present for every decision, every shift, every crisis. That's not a restaurant. That's a hostage situation with a liquor license.

If you're reading this and you recognize yourself — you're not lazy. You're not failing. You're running a system that was never designed to run without you.

That's a solvable problem. But not by working harder.

I write about this every week. Link in bio if you want the newsletter.

---

**Structural notes for SKILL.md:**
- Hook: one vivid scene (3 seconds of falling asleep on the line)
- Story arc: specific moment > context (hours, timeline) > pattern recognition > reframe
- Reframe: personal failure > system failure
- Tone: vulnerable but not self-pitying. Direct. German-direct.
- CTA: soft (newsletter), not a tool or call
- Length: ~200 words. Short paragraphs. Fragments.

**Pain point:** Staff Chaos
**Awareness stage:** Problem Aware
**Hook type:** Question
**CTA destination:** Newsletter / Substack (via conversation)

---

If your best cook quit tomorrow, how long until the kitchen falls apart?

I asked myself that question 3 years ago. The honest answer was: by lunch.

Not because the team was bad. Because everything — every recipe, every prep list, every decision about what to order and when — lived in one person's head. Mine.

That's not a team. That's a single point of failure wearing chef whites.

The fix wasn't hiring better people. It was writing things down. Prep systems. Close-down checklists. Par sheets that any cook could follow on day one.

Boring? Yes. But my new-hire training went from 3 weeks to 4 days. And the kitchen didn't collapse the first time I took a sick day.

What's your version of this? If you disappeared for a week, what breaks first?

---

**Structural notes for SKILL.md:**
- Hook: question that creates an uncomfortable answer
- Story: personal, specific (honest answer was "by lunch")
- Reframe: not a people problem, a documentation problem
- Fix: simple, boring, effective
- Close: question back to the group (engagement driver)
- Tone: peer conversation, not coaching. No "I can help you" — just the story.
- No CTA link. The value IS the conversation. Profile click is the conversion.

## Example 9: Reddit Field Report — BURNOUT (Cross-platform)

PLACEHOLDER — Replace with approved post

**Pain point:** Exhaustion / Burnout
**Awareness stage:** Problem Aware > Solution Aware
**Hook type:** Failure / Vulnerability (long form)
**CTA destination:** None (Reddit = no pitch. Profile stalking is the conversion.)

---

**Title:** I burned out twice running restaurants. Here's what I actually changed the second time.

**Body:**

First time: ran a restaurant in Australia for 3 years. 80-hour weeks. Lost my relationship. Lost 12kg. Told myself it was the cost of doing business.

I didn't change anything. I just moved countries and did it again.

Second time: running my own place in Germany. Same hours. Same chaos. Same "just until things stabilize." Except this time I was also $370K CAD in debt and watching my marriage crack.

What I changed (the boring version):

1. I wrote down every prep task. Every single one. Tied each to a par level and a sales forecast. Took a weekend. Boring. But suddenly any cook could prep without asking me 15 questions.

2. I stopped doing inventory from memory. Made a par sheet. Ordering went from "what do I think we need" to "what does the sheet say." Took 20 minutes to build. Cut food waste noticeably within 2 weeks.

3. I built a close-down checklist. Not a fancy one. A laminated piece of paper. Before that, I was the only person who could close properly. After, anyone could.

That's it. No software. No consultant. No "mindset shift." Three documents that meant I didn't have to be there for every decision.

My hours went from 80 to about 50 within a month. Then 45 over the next two months as the team got used to the systems.

I'm not going to pretend I'm fully "recovered." But I sleep. I see my family. And the restaurant runs the same whether I'm there or not.

If you're in the thick of it right now: you don't need motivation. You need to write things down. Start with the prep list.

Happy to share the templates if anyone wants them.

---

**Structural notes for SKILL.md:**
- Title: specific, honest, no clickbait
- Opening: two burnouts, not one. Shows the pattern.
- "What I changed": deliberately boring. Three documents. No magic.
- Tone: Reddit-native. No marketing language. No frameworks. Just "here's what I did."
- Close: offer to share, not a link. Let mods and community decide.
- Length: 300-400 words. Longer than social, shorter than a blog.
- Rule: Reddit rewards honesty and specificity. Zero pitch. Zero links in the body.

## APPROVED Example: LinkedIn Text Post — LEADERSHIP / AUTHORITY

**APPROVED** — Real post by Chris. Use as primary voice reference.

**Pain point:** Leadership / Authority (applies to operator mindset)
**Awareness stage:** Unaware → Problem Aware
**Hook type:** Story / Observation
**CTA destination:** Organic engagement (no tool CTA — pure value post)
**Trinity voice:** Sage (primary), Creator (secondary)
**Pillar:** Systematic Excellence

---

I watched two chefs destroy each other over a title nobody offered them.

Early in my career. Hotel kitchen. I was a young Commis.

The Sous Chef position was empty.
Two senior guys had been there for years.
Both decided the job was theirs.

But neither of them stepped up.

They didn't run better service.
They didn't teach the junior cooks.
They didn't solve problems.

They just started telling people what to do.

"Do this." "I'm in charge." "Listen to me."

The kitchen turned into a war zone.
Team morale collapsed.
People stopped caring.

And the result?

Neither got the job.
The team wouldn't follow either of them.

Because here's the thing about authority in a kitchen:

You can't claim it.
You can't demand it.
You can only earn it.

The chef who stays late to prep for the guy
who's drowning — that's authority.

The chef who teaches the new kid how to
hold a knife properly — that's authority.

The chef who runs service clean when
everything's falling apart — that's authority.

Title follows value. Never the other way around.

I've run kitchens for 20 years since then.
The best leaders I've ever worked with
never once asked for the title.

They just did the work.
And everyone followed.

Stop chasing positions.
Start providing value.
The title will find you.

---

**Structural notes for SKILL.md:**
- Hook: vivid scene from early career (young Commis watching two seniors self-destruct)
- Story arc: observation > what they did wrong > consequence > reframe > universal lesson
- No dollar signs — this is a pure value/engagement post, not profit-focused
- Rhythm: short lines, almost poetic. Line breaks used for pacing, not paragraphs.
- The repetition pattern ("You can't claim it. You can't demand it. You can only earn it.") is a signature Sage move
- Three parallel examples of real authority — each one line, each concrete
- Closing: three short imperatives. No CTA link. The post IS the value.
- Voice: 100% Sage. Fatherly, gruff, earned wisdom. No sarcasm (Rebel absent). No systems talk (Creator light).
- This is the model for "pure engagement" posts in the 50-30-20 mix (30% engagement bucket)

2004 — 2019

The Climb.
15 Years of Kitchen Grind.

Started with nothing. Black Forest kid who wanted to cook. Worked every station. Every country that would take me. Every position from the bottom up. No shortcuts. No connections. Just showed up and outworked the room.

2004 — 2007
The Foundation
Apprentice in the Black Forest, Germany. Hotelfachschule Villingen. Steigenberger Academy. Learned the German way: discipline first, creativity later. No excuses accepted. Standards beaten into muscle memory.
Schwarzwaldhotel Rothaus Steigenberger Academy
2007 — 2014
Working Through Europe
Germany. Switzerland. Austria. Spain. Every kitchen taught something different. Alpine precision. Mediterranean chaos. High-volume banquet operations. Boutique fine dining. Moved through Commis, Demi Chef, Chef Tournant, Sous Chef. Learned to run any kitchen you put me in.
Restaurant Wildstrubel, Switzerland Hotel Tres Playa, Mallorca
2014 — 2017
Canada — The Yukon
Landed in Dawson City, Yukon. Population 1,300. End of the road. Literally. Ran the kitchen at Aurora Inn. Supply chain meant weekly trucks on a dirt highway. Staff meant whoever showed up. Extreme conditions. Zero margin for error.
Aurora Inn, Dawson City Yukon, Canada
2017
Grumpy Schnitzel
Founded my own restaurant in Dawson City. Built it from nothing. German-inspired comfort food at the edge of the world. One small room, one German chef, one shot. It worked. For a while.
Grumpy Schnitzel, Dawson City Owner / Operator

"I thought if I just worked harder, everything would hold together. That's what they tell you in this industry. Work harder. Push through. Sleep later. Nobody tells you that's the road to losing everything."

-- Christian Schiffner

2019 — 2022

The Fall.
$180K and Everything Else.

This is the part most people skip. The part where it all came apart. I'm leading with it because it's the only part that matters.

2019
The "Growth" Trap
Bought a second restaurant. Because that's what you do when things are working, right? Scale. Grow. More revenue. More seats. More staff. More problems I couldn't see coming because I was too tired to look.
2019 — 2021
The Death Spiral
80-100 hour weeks. Every week. Food cost? Untracked. Systems? Nonexistent. Staff retention? A joke. My answer to every problem: work harder. Stay later. Sleep less. I'll figure it out tomorrow. Tomorrow never came.
2021 — 2022
$180K in Debt
The money was gone. Not slowly. All at once, it felt like. But it had been bleeding out for years. I just couldn't see it because I was too busy running between two kitchens, putting out fires with no water left.
April 2022
Rock Bottom
Lost the restaurant. Lost the money. Lost myself. That's not a metaphor. When you work 100 hours a week for three years and watch it all disappear, you don't just lose a business. You lose the story you told yourself about who you are.
The Turning Point

You Can't Hustle Your Way
to Profitability.

Recovery taught me something the restaurant industry never did.

The Revelation
Same Problem, Different Arena
You can't motivation your way through addiction. You need systems. Frameworks. Pre-decided responses that hold when your willpower is gone. And then it hit me: that's exactly what my restaurant needed. Not more hustle. Not more hours. Systems that work when I'm exhausted. Because I'm always exhausted in this business.
The Core Framework
Behavior Leads Belief
Not the other way around. You don't wait until you "feel motivated" to track food cost. You build the system. You follow the system. The results change the belief. Recovery works this way. Business works this way. Everything works this way. The industry just hasn't figured it out yet.
The Decision
Same Frameworks. Different Problem.
If recovery frameworks can keep someone sober through the worst days of their life, they can keep a restaurant profitable through a Tuesday night with two no-shows and a broken dishwasher. I was going to prove it.

"You can't motivation your way through addiction. You can't hustle your way to profitability. You need systems that hold when you're at 60% capacity. Because that's where you live most days."

-- Christian Schiffner, 1,200+ days later
